# Economics

## Protocol Fees

Every protocol in The Substrate charges an immutable fee on settlement. The fee range across the suite is 0.3% to 0.5% of transaction value. This is set at deployment and cannot be changed by anyone, including the original author.

This is not a business decision. It is an architectural one.

Infrastructure should not be a rent-seeking layer. The protocol fee is set at the level required to sustain the treasury that funds ongoing public infrastructure — no more. It is the cost of using neutral, ownerless, permanent settlement rails. It is not a mechanism for extracting value from the economic activity that happens on top of them.

---

## The Extractive Ceiling

The immutable protocol fee creates a permanent economic ceiling on extraction across the entire Substrate ecosystem.

Any platform built on The Substrate that attempts to charge extractive fees is always competing against the template path — where the only fee is the protocol fee itself. Users and builders who find a platform's margins unreasonable have a documented, open, permanently available alternative. The economics of overreach do not work when the exit is always visible and always free.

This is not enforced by governance, by legal agreement, or by any central authority. It is enforced by arithmetic. The moment a platform's fee structure becomes irrational relative to the protocol fee, the rational move is to use the templates directly. That option never closes.

---

## The Anti-Capture Structure

The Substrate's resistance to capture rests on three properties that operate independently and reinforce each other:

**Immutable protocol fees**: the economic layer cannot be re-priced by any actor. No acquirer, no regulator, no platform can raise the cost of using the protocol itself.

**Open templates**: the reference implementation is always public, always current, always deployable. There is no proprietary access layer that can be bought, closed, or gated.

**Open source**: the full protocol logic is visible and auditable by anyone. There is no obscured behavior that a controlling party could exploit.

Each of these properties alone limits capture. Together they make it structurally impossible. An actor who controls one controls nothing, because the other two remain intact.

This was not designed as a defensive posture. It is simply what neutral public infrastructure looks like when built correctly. Capture resistance is a consequence of the architecture, not a feature added on top of it.

For context, the centralized infrastructure handling equivalent transactions today charges: 1.5–3.5% for payment rails (Visa, Mastercard), 2.9% for digital payments (PayPal, Stripe), 8–15% for marketplace logistics (Amazon), 20–30% for delivery platforms, and 10–20% for asset transfer including dealer margins. The Substrate's protocol fees are 0.3–0.5%. The difference is not marginal. It is structural.

The fee differential between DeDe and DROP is intentional. It creates a natural economic incentive for platforms to combine both protocols. the blended fee across a combined DeDe + DROP workflow is lower than DeDe alone at scale, rewarding deeper integration with the Substrate suite.

## Summary

Low immutable fees and open templates are not gestures toward fairness. They are the mechanism by which The Substrate remains what it is, neutral, ownerless, permanently accessible infrastructure, regardless of what is built on top of it or who tries to control it.

The economics are the architecture. They cannot be separated.
