# Privacy

## The Existing Framework

Humanity has already settled the question of privacy. The Substrate does not introduce a new privacy position. It aligns with the one that already exists.

**Universal Declaration of Human Rights, Article 12:**
> No one shall be subjected to arbitrary interference with his privacy, family, home or correspondence.

**European Convention on Human Rights, Article 8:**
> Everyone has the right to respect for his private and family life, his home and his correspondence.

**General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR):**
Data minimization. Purpose limitation. No unnecessary collection of personal information. The right to not be tracked beyond what a transaction requires.

These are not suggestions. They are the agreed baseline of civilized society, encoded in international law, regional law, and national law across the majority of the world.

---

## Parcels Are Correspondence

A parcel dispatched between two parties, with no central record of who sent what to whom, is functionally identical to a sealed letter. The legal and moral precedent for protecting that is centuries old.

DeDe is a correspondence protocol. The pickup and dropoff data is encrypted. No identity is required beyond what the transacting parties choose to disclose to each other. No central authority holds a record of the exchange. No platform monetizes the movement data.

This is not a privacy feature. It is the default state, the same default that applies to every letter ever sent.

Anyone who argues that this level of privacy is inappropriate is arguing against Article 12 of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights. That is the correct framing of the conversation.

---

## Privacy Across The Substrate

The same principle extends across all six protocols.

No protocol in The Substrate requires identity beyond what the transaction itself demands. No protocol stores data centrally. No protocol has a platform layer that can surveil, extract, or monetize the behavior of its users. The deployer controls their own data. The user controls their own disclosure.

This is GDPR compliant by architecture, not by policy. There is no personal data to protect because the system is not designed to collect it.

## The Reference Framework

The Substrate's privacy model is grounded in and consistent with:

- Universal Declaration of Human Rights, Article 12 (1948)
- European Convention on Human Rights, Article 8 (1950)
- International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights, Article 17 (1966)
- General Data Protection Regulation, GDPR (EU, 2018)
- California Consumer Privacy Act, CCPA (US, 2020)
- Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act, PIPEDA (Canada)
- Lei Geral de Proteção de Dados, LGPD (Brazil, 2020)
- Privacy Act (Australia, 1988)

This is not an exhaustive list. It is an illustration that the direction of global legal development is consistent and unambiguous: individuals have the right to private communication and the right not to be surveilled beyond what they have consented to.

The Substrate is built in that direction.

---

## Summary

The right to private correspondence is not a crypto argument. It is a human rights argument. The Substrate implements it technically. The existing legal framework defends it politically and legally.

There is nothing to argue about. The framework already exists. The Substrate simply respects it.
